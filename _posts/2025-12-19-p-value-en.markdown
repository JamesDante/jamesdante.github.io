In survey and questionnaire research, you frequently encounter hypotheses. Why hypotheses? Because the real world is full of complexity and uncertainty — hypotheses are a rigorous, scientific way to express results.

You want to know: **"Does the world I'm observing look like the world described by my hypothesis?"** After running a large-scale study, the results are typically one of two things:

- No, it doesn't — the null hypothesis is probably false.
- It might — we simply can't refute it.

To keep this post readable, I'll avoid mathematical formulas as much as possible.

**Alternative Hypothesis (Ha)**: Predicts that a significant difference exists.

**Null Hypothesis (H0)**: Predicts that no difference exists.

> Example: Suppose you're testing whether a new drug effectively lowers blood pressure.
> Ha: The new drug has an effect on blood pressure.
> H0: The new drug has no effect on blood pressure.

**p-value**: In hypothesis testing, the p-value helps you decide whether to reject the null hypothesis. In plain terms, it's a probability — the smaller it is, the more inconsistent the observed result is with the null hypothesis, and the stronger the case for rejecting it.

> Using the example above: the smaller the p-value, the more likely it is that "this drug has no effect on blood pressure" is false.

But how small is small enough? That's where the concept of significance level comes in.

**Significance Level (α)**: Typically set at 0.05, 0.01, or 0.10. This is the threshold for rejecting the null hypothesis. Why these values? Mostly convention — everyone uses them, so they stuck.

> p < α: Reject the null hypothesis; the observed effect is statistically significant.
> p ≥ α: Fail to reject the null hypothesis; not enough evidence to conclude an effect exists.

So how is the p-value actually calculated? Common methods include the z-test, t-test, and chi-square test. I won't go into detail here, but you can explore [the z-test](https://medium.com/@jamesdante/null-hypothesis-alternative-hypothesis-and-p-value-81b1997a15e0) and [the chi-square test](https://www.shuxuele.com/data/chi-square-test.html) further.

The core logic behind all these methods is: **"Given that the null hypothesis is true, what is the probability of observing a result at least as extreme as this one?"** That probability is the p-value — and it's precisely why a very small p-value leads us to reject the null.

Each method targets a different type of question:

Chi-square test: Does a distribution match expectations? Typical scenario: Is gender related to whether someone has a disease?
z-test / t-test: Is there a difference in means? Typical scenario: Is the average blood pressure different between two groups?

z-test: Parameters are known; you're just verifying deviation.
t-test: Even the scale itself is being estimated.

Accordingly, they apply to different data types:
Chi-square test: Categorical data — e.g., male/female, yes/no, A/B/C
z-test / t-test: Continuous data — e.g., blood pressure, height, test scores
